CPU

The CPU, or Central Processing Unit, is essentially the brain of the computer. The CPU's job is to execute instructions in the form of code. The CPU consists of an ALU, or arithmic and logic unit, a control unit, and a varying number of registers. Modern CPU's use multiple cores designed to seperate tasks and thus run more efficiently. Thus, more cores = more processing power. Generally Hexa Cores and higher are only used in servers or quantum computers.

To see your PC's CPU, go into Command Prompt and type wmic cpu get caption.

  1. Single Core
  2. Dual Core
  3. Quad Core
  4. Hexa Core
  5. Octa Core
  6. Deca Core
GPU

The GPU, or Graphics Processing Unit, is what handles graphics. Designed to rapidly manipulate memory to accelerate the pixelation of images to match target framerates, these are an important aspect of any computer built for gaming or utilizing HD multimedia.

To check your PC's GPU open Command Prompt and type in wmic path win32_VideoController get name.

  1. Integrated

    These graphics are built in to the motherboard, and are generally less equipped for intensive computing (gaming.)

  2. Discrete

    These are seperate GPU's, attached externally to the motherboard, and are generally capable for higher graphics quality at higher framerates.

Motherboard

The Motherboard is the backbone of the PC, it is the framework that binds together most of the other elements of a computer. When you think of the inside of a computer, you probably think of a lot of chips and wires - this is the motherboard, connecting everything together. The primary difference between types of motherboards is varying ports, both external and internal, among other small variations.

To see your motherboard specs, open Command Prompt and type in wmic baseboard get product,Manufacturer.

  1. AT
  2. ATX
  3. LPX
  4. BTX
  5. Pico BTX
  6. Mini ITX
Memory

Your RAM, or random access memory, is your PC's short-term memory. It remembers and files everything currently running on your computer. All data currently being accessed is stored and organized in the RAM, so that your computer can run various operations or files at the same time. The two primary variations in RAM types are DRAM, Dynamic RAM, and SRAM, Static RAM. DRAM, given to small drains in information stored, has to be refreshed every few milliseconds, thus making it slightly slower than SRAM.

To check your PC's RAM, open Command Prompt and type in wmic MEMORYCHIP get BankLabel, DeviceLocator, Capacity, Speed.

  1. SRAM
  2. DRAM
  3. SDRAM
  4. SDR SDRAM
  5. DDR, DDR2, DDR3, DDR4
  6. GDDR. GDDR2, GDDR3, GDDR4, GDDR5
  7. Flash Memory
Hard Drive

The Hard Drive is long-term memory. This is the physical space in which all your files are stored, from pictures to music to games to movies, it all gets stored on the Hard Drive, which can be accessed by your computer, or more specifically your OS, or Operations System, as needed.

To see details on your Hard Drive, open Command Prompt and type wmic diskdrive for general specs, or wmic diskdrive get size for size specifically.

  1. PATA

    Parallel ATA, a traditional and mechanical Hard Drive, rarely used in modern PC's.

  2. SATA

    Serial ATA, much like a PATA, except for differences in encoding and transportation, these are also mechanical Hard Drive's, and the default for most modern PC's.

  3. SSD

    Unlike the mechanical Hard Drives mentioned above, a Solid State Drive stores data on interconnected flash drives, as opposed to the various platters used on older HDD's, therefore making it able to transfer data significantly faster.

Cooling System

Your PC, or more specifically certain parts of your PC, get very hot while running. Specifically, your CPU and GPU (especially during intensive computing such as gaming) generate a lot of heat, but the entire inner workings of a computer get hot, and this affects performance and could potentially damange the delicate parts inside. The solution? Cooling, of course. While CPU's and GPU's typically have their own cooling facilities, fans will generally be used to cycle fresh air in and through the PC to help keep the inside as cool as possible.

To find out more about your PC's cooling system, you'll have to either access a detailed specs sheet for your computer or open up the tower yourself and examine.

  1. Heat Sinks

    Typically used on CPU's, although most modern gaming rigs have liquid cooling for the CPU as well.

  2. Fans

    Most PC's have some sort of fan system to cycle air through the inner workings and over the parts, thus taking with them some of the heat generated through computing. More advanced PC's, such as those used for gaming, will contain 6 or more fans, whereras standard PC's will often only have one or two.

  3. Liquid Cooling

    Primarily used for gaming PC's (and some servers and quantum computers) these systems have hoses that flow through the PC and over heat-generating parts specifically, and run fluid through the hose/tube system while cycling through a cooler. This is much how any liquid cooled engine or radiator would run.
